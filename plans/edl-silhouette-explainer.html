EDL silhouettes: near-side vs far-side darkening

A picture-first walkthrough of the choice in EDL.frag — what each option does, why the point‑cloud ↔ point‑cloud and point‑cloud ↔ opaque edges currently disagree, and the trade‑offs of each fix.

1. What EDL is actually doing

Eye-Dome Lighting darkens a pixel based on how its depth compares to its 8 screen-space neighbors. For every neighbor it accumulates a depth gap into a response, then: 

// bigger response  →  darker pixel
shade = exp(-response * strength);
fragColor.rgb = cloud.rgb * shade;

The only real decision is: at a depth cliff, which of the two pixels do we darken — the one in front (nearer the camera) or the one behind (farther)? That single choice is the whole topic.

2. Anatomy of a depth cliff

These figures are a side-on slice. Up = closer to the camera, down = farther away. Left-to-right is position across your screen. Two screen-adjacent pixels straddle the edge: the near pixel sits on the front surface, the far pixel on the surface behind it.

3. The two choices, side by side

The intuition: far-side darkening sinks shadow into the recesses of a surface (like ambient occlusion in a crevice) — it makes a single continuous cloud read as a 3D shape. Near-side darkening draws an ink outline around whatever is poking toward you — it makes an object pop against what's behind it.

Front view now (what you'd actually see). Picture a blob of cloud in front of something farther away.

5. Why your two cases disagree today

Here's the crux. The current shader doesn't choose near vs far by geometry — it chooses by what kind of neighbor sits across the cliff: 

if (neighbor is cloud)   shapeResponse      += max(0, logZ₀ − logZᵢ);   // FAR-side
else /* opaque/bg */     silhouetteResponse += clamp(logZᵢ − logZ₀, 0, c); // NEAR-side

Same physical situation — a cloud surface in front of something farther — but the outline jumps to the opposite side of the cliff depending on whether the thing behind is cloud or opaque. On top of that the two branches use different strengths and only one is clamped. That's the inconsistency you're seeing.

An important asymmetry you can't escape

At a cloud ↔ opaque edge the cloud is always the near side (if opaque were in front, the cloud would be occluded and wouldn't be drawn at all). And we can only ever shade cloud pixels in this pass. So against opaque geometry or the background, near-side is the only outline that's physically possible — far-side would try to paint a non-cloud pixel and produce nothing. The realistic way to make the two cases match is therefore to bring the near-side outline to the cloud ↔ cloud case too.

6. The three options

Option A — Near outline plus far shape near far

Apply the near-side silhouette to every farther neighbor (cloud or not) and keep the far-side shape cue for closer cloud neighbors. An internal cloud cliff then gets a near-lip outline (matching the cloud ↔ opaque look) and retains its interior far-lip shading.

  • Pro: silhouettes are consistent everywhere; you keep the classic EDL 3D-shape cue inside a cloud.
  • Con: internal cliffs get darkened on both lips, so busy clouds look a touch heavier / "outliney".

Option B — Near outline only near

Drop the far-side shape term entirely; darken purely by the near-side gap against any farther neighbor.

  • Pro: simplest, perfectly symmetric — one term, one strength, every edge treated identically.
  • Con: you lose the CloudCompare/Potree interior depth cue. A single smooth cloud surface flattens out; only its outer silhouette reads. For dense LiDAR this is usually a real downgrade.

Option C — Match strength/clamp only far + near (sides unchanged)

Keep the current near/far split by neighbor type, but make both terms share one strength and one clamp so at least the darkness matches.

  • Pro: smallest change; equalizes brightness.
  • Con: doesn't fix the real problem — the outline still sits on opposite sides of the cliff in the two cases. They'll be equally dark but still visibly different.

7. At a glance

Option Internal cloud cliff Cloud vs opaque/bg Interior 3D cue kept? Consistent?
A. Near + far near outline + far shading near outline Yes Yes (outline matches; cliffs are heavier)
B. Near only near outline near outline No Yes (fully symmetric, flatter)
C. Match strength far shading near outline Yes No (same darkness, opposite sides)

My recommendation: Option A. It's the only one that makes the silhouette behave the same in both cases without throwing away the interior shape shading that makes a point cloud readable as 3D. The cost is purely that internal depth cliffs pick up an extra near-side outline — tunable down via silhouetteStrength if it reads too heavy. Option B is the move only if you decide you don't want interior EDL shading at all.

8. The knobs, for reference

Both terms feed the same shade = exp(-response * k). The CPU side (cwEDLEffect) exposes: m_strengthBaseline (1500, far-side shape, depth-normalized), m_silhouetteStrength (3.0, near-side outline, raw), and m_silhouetteClamp (1.0, caps how dark a single very-distant neighbor can drive an edge so a far background saturates instead of going pure black). Whichever option we pick, these stay the tuning surface.
